Introduction to Networking

Networking is the practice of connecting computers and other devices together to share resources and information. It forms the foundation of modern communication, allowing devices to interact locally and globally through wired and wireless technologies.

Types of Networks

Networks vary in scale and purpose. Below are the four primary types:

LAN (Local Area Network)
A small network covering a limited area such as a home, school, or office building.
WAN (Wide Area Network)
A network that covers large geographic areas, often connecting multiple LANs, like the internet.
MAN (Metropolitan Area Network)
Spans a city or a large campus, connecting multiple LANs within a metropolitan area.
PAN (Personal Area Network)
A small network around a single person, typically involving personal devices like phones or laptops.

Network Topologies

Network topology refers to the arrangement of elements (links, nodes, etc.) in a communication network. Common types include:

  • Star: All devices connect to a central hub or switch.
  • Bus: All nodes share a single communication line.
  • Ring: Each device connects to two others forming a circular path.
  • Mesh: Devices are interconnected for redundancy and fault tolerance.

Networking Technologies

Various technologies enable networking, such as Ethernet (wired), Wi-Fi (wireless), and Bluetooth (short-range communication). Modern advancements include 5G, cloud networking, and virtualization, which enhance connectivity and scalability.

Networking Tools

  • Ping: Tests connectivity between devices.
  • Traceroute: Displays the path data takes across a network.
  • Wireshark: Captures and analyzes network packets.
  • Network Tester: Checks cable integrity and signal strength.
